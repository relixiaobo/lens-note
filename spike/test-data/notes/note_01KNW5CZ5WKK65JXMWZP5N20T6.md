The claim that learning ease depends on prior knowledge structure has an asymmetric implication that's easy to miss: knowledge networks are not additive, they're multiplicative. A person with a dense, well-structured prior network doesn't just learn slightly faster — they have exponentially more 'hooks' on which new concepts can hang. This means early investment in foundational structure compounds, while fragmented knowledge accumulation plateaus. The structure of what you know matters more than the quantity of what you know.
