The commercial viability problem for AI browsers like Dia can be decomposed into three compounding structural disadvantages: (1) incumbent subsidization — Google/Apple/Microsoft fund browsers from advertising and OS profits, making a standalone browser's pricing logic untenable; (2) distribution lock-in — preinstallation gives incumbents default status that no startup can overcome with a marginally better UX; (3) habit gravity — browsers are habitual infrastructure, not chosen products. Taken together, these three factors mean the AI browser space is not a market where better product wins. It is a market where the structural winner was already set before AI existed. This is a sharper, more operational articulation of the 'distribution wrapper without moat' diagnosis already in note_01KNVM1WC9B4VKECN2VPE8YVE7.
