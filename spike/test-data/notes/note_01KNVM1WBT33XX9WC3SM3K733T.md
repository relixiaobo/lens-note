The claim that 'multi-agent systems work mainly because they help spend enough tokens to solve the problem' is a deflating but important mechanistic explanation. It reframes multi-agent architectures not as coordination triumphs but as compute-allocation strategies: the real scarce resource is inference compute, and parallelism is just a way to spend more of it. This aligns with the Bitter Lesson's deeper point — computation wins, and multi-agent is yet another mechanism to throw more computation at a problem.
