The real cost of reusing old notes is not retrieval — it's context reconstruction. An old note embeds the cognitive context of the moment it was written: what problem was being worked on, what had just been read, what question was live. At retrieval time, that context has evaporated, and reconstructing it (or bridging it to the new situation) often costs more effort than simply starting fresh with new sources. This is why old notes feel like 'junk' — not because the content is bad, but because the context is missing. This maps to a broader principle: knowledge artifacts without preserved context degrade faster than knowledge in living documents or conversations. The implication is that the most valuable thing to capture alongside a note is not the content but the *occasion* — why this, why now.
