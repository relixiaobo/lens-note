The meta-prompt's 'Minimal Changes' doctrine — improve simple prompts freely, but for complex prompts only enhance clarity without altering structure — encodes a conservatism principle analogous to Chesterton's Fence applied to prompts. For complex prompts, the existing structure likely encodes implicit knowledge: task decomposition choices, ordering decisions, and behavioral constraints whose rationale may not be visible from the surface. Freely restructuring a complex prompt is like removing a fence you don't understand — you may eliminate load-bearing constraints that aren't labeled as such. This is distinct from the KB's notes about prompt stability under context growth (note_01KNW5ZVD88Y3NBSKB7MVE148J); that note is about prompts degrading over time, while this principle is about humans degrading prompts through well-intentioned editing.
